AUDIO DATA
A sound wave, like any other wave, is introduced into a medium by a vibrating object. The vibrating object is the source of the disturbance that moves through the medium. The vibrating object that creates the disturbance could be the vocal cords of a person, the vibrating string and sound board of a guitar or violin.
Frequency (pitch)—
The frequency of a wave refers to how often the particles of the medium vibrate when a wave passes through the medium. The frequency of a wave is measured as the number of complete back-and-forth vibrations of a particle of the medium per unit of time. A commonly used unit for frequency is the Hertz (abbreviated Hz), where 1 Hertz = 1 vibration/second
Amplitude (loudness)—
The amplitude determines a sound's volume — sound waves of higher amplitude are louder. For example, the sound wave created by an theatre actor projecting their lines to the audience has a higher amplitude than the sound wave produced by someone in the audience whispering to their friend.
An Equalizer —
An equalizer is the traditional audiovisualization for frequency distribution of audio data. In the visual to the right, the Fast Fourier Transform (FFT) algorithm converts an audio signal from its original domain (time) into a representation of the frequency domain. In other words, FFT separates the audio data total amplitude into the amplitude across frequencies.
